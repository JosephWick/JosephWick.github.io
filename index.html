About Me

In October 2023, I began a position in the CPAC group at Argonne National Lab. I work closely with Dr. Matthew Becker in the development of differentiable code, which allows for faster optimization and, as a result, more complicated models. I completed my masters in Scientific Computing and Applied Mathematics at UCSC in the summer of 2023. As part of the SciCAM program, I am wrote my thesis on analyzing H1 simulations of dwarf galaxies ran under both self-interacting dark matter (SIDM) and lambda cold dark matter (LCDM). My goal is to determine observable differences in the galaxies across the two different paradigms, information which can then be used in follow up H1 observations. My work is part of Professor Leauthaud's Merian Survey, which seeks to test SIDM by performing gravitational weak lensing measurements on dwarf galaxies.

Prior to my graduate education, I studied physics and computer science at the undergraduate level, graduating in 2022. As an undergraduate, I worked on some data aspects of the Merian survey with Professor Leauthaud, and observed remotely on the Keck and Blanco telescopes. I also spent a summer as an intern at the Southern California Earthquake Center, where I implemented hierarchical matrices into a simulation of earthquakes and aseismic slip (SEAS), and attended the annual SCEC and Seismological Society of America conferences.

I first became involved in applied math and computational physics through an interest in software development, and I still enjoy working on small personal projects. When I'm not busy applying the math or furiously typing, I also enjoy the outside world. I am a fan of both watching and playing soccer, riding bikes, competing in collegiate triathlons, and I recently learned how to sew.
